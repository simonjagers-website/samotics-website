## Voice Model

The Samotics voice balances engineering authority with commercial clarity. Think: calm, confident, precise. Not: breathless, salesy, vague.

We write as a trusted technical partner, not a vendor pitching a product.

### Four Registers

Every sentence on the site operates in one of four registers. Most pages blend two or three.

**1. Technically Rigorous.** Engineer-to-engineer. Grounded in physics, specific in terminology.

- Do: Use specific parameters. "20 kHz sampling frequency." "90% detection accuracy."
- Don't: Use vague qualifiers. "High-speed," "very accurate," "smart data."

> Example: "Traditional vibration sensors require waterproofing and cabling that often fail in sewage. SAM4 monitors current and voltage from the MCC, bypassing the submerged environment entirely."

**2. Commercially Clear.** Translate technical capability into business outcomes.

- Do: Name the outcome. "200% ROI in year one." "€650,000 saved per avoided shutdown."
- Don't: Explain FFT to someone asking about budget.

> Example: "By detecting 9 out of 10 failures in advance, SAM4 lets maintenance teams shift from reactive repairs to planned interventions. The result: 200% ROI in the first year."

**3. Restrained and Authoritative.** No hype. Results speak. Admit limitations honestly.

- Do: Use affirmative sentences. State what the system does, with evidence.
- Don't: Use "revolutionary," "best-in-class," "game-changing," or exclamation marks.

> Example: "SAM4 detects over 90% of developing faults. While no system is perfect, we maintain a false alarm rate below 1%."

> Example (limitations): "ESA requires a minimum rotational speed of 450 RPM. For machines with high load variations, a technical review is necessary to ensure accuracy."

**4. Direct and Purposeful.** Respect the user's time. Every sentence has a job.

- Do: Front-load the important information. Use imperatives for instructions.
- Don't: Write introductory paragraphs about "today's digital landscape."

> Example: "Book a demo." "Monitor hard-to-reach assets." "Enter your work email to receive the whitepaper."

## House Style

These rules apply to all website content. No exceptions unless technical accuracy requires it.

### Sentence and Paragraph Rules

Web users read roughly 20% of the words on a page. Write for scanning.

| Rule | Target |
|---|---|
| Sentence length | Average 15–20 words. Avoid exceeding 25. |
| Paragraph length | 1–3 sentences. Never more than 4. |
| Subordinate clauses | One per sentence maximum. |
| Transitions | Use logical connectors ("Consequently," "In contrast") at sentence starts. |

### Active Voice

Default to active voice. The subject performs the verb.

| Passive (avoid) | Active (use) |
|---|---|
| "A reduction in energy waste is facilitated by ESA." | "ESA reduces energy waste." |
| "Bearing wear is identified by spectral analysis." | "Spectral analysis identifies bearing wear." |
| "The identification of faults is performed by the system." | "The system identifies faults." |

**Exceptions:** Use passive when the actor is genuinely unknown or the object matters more than the actor. "The motor was replaced" is fine if the focus is on the motor's lifecycle.

**Kill zombie nouns.** Ruthlessly prune nouns made from verbs: "implementation" → implement, "utilization" → use, "optimization" → optimize, "detection" → detect.

### Punctuation

**Em-dashes:** Limit to one per page section at most. Prefer a period, colon, comma, or parenthetical instead. Em-dashes are legitimate punctuation, but overuse signals loose sentence construction. If you find yourself reaching for an em-dash, ask whether a full stop and a new sentence would be clearer. Usually it is.

| With em-dash (avoid) | Preferred alternative |
|---|---|
| "SAM4 monitors from the cabinet — no sensors on the asset." | "SAM4 monitors from the cabinet. No sensors on the asset." |
| "The system detects faults — including bearing wear and misalignment." | "The system detects faults, including bearing wear and misalignment." |
| "ESA captures data from the MCC — the motor control cabinet." | "ESA captures data from the MCC (the motor control cabinet)." |

**Semicolons:** Use sparingly. Most semicolon sentences are better as two separate sentences.

**Colons:** Good for introducing a list, a result, or a specification. "The result: 200% ROI."

### Headlines and Hierarchy

Headings are the most-read elements on any page. They must work on their own.

- **Capitalization:** Sentence case only. Capitalize the first word and proper nouns.
- **Keywords first:** Front-load the most important word.
- **Levels:** H1 for page title, H2 for sections, H3 for sub-points. Never skip a level.
- **Be descriptive, not clever.** No puns. No "marketing speak."

| Weak | Strong |
|---|---|
| "A New Way to Monitor Your Submerged Assets" | "Monitor Submerged Assets Without Sensors" |
| "The Future of Maintenance" | "Sensorless Condition Monitoring" |
| "General Information" | "Monitor Submerged Pumps" |

### Jargon Rules

We follow a three-tier system. The goal: maintain credibility with engineers while staying accessible to executives.

| Tier | Category | Rule | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Universal industrial | Use freely. No definition needed. | Motor, pump, downtime, ROI, uptime |
| 2 | Industry specific | Define on first use with a plain-English equivalent. | ESA (Electrical Signature Analysis), MCC (Motor Control Cabinet), VFD |
| 3 | Expert only | Use only in technical docs and blogs. Define or link to glossary. | FFT, harmonic distortion, stator winding insulation |

### Acronyms and Naming

Consistency matters more than you think. Follow these without exception.

| Element | Rule | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Acronyms | Spell out on first use per page. | "Electrical Signature Analysis (ESA)" |
| Company name | "Samotics." Never "Samotics B.V." or "The Samotics Team" in body copy. | |
| Product name | "SAM4" (all caps, no space). Use "SAM4 Health" or "SAM4 Energy" when specific. | |
| Possessives | Avoid "Samotics's." Use "Samotics'" or rephrase. | "Samotics' insights" or "insights from Samotics" |
| Hardware | "DAQ device," "Connectivity Kit," "Motor Kit." Capitalize consistently. | |

### Numbers and Claims

Numbers build trust. Adjectives don't.

- **Format:** Use digits for all numbers (5, 10, 50) except when starting a sentence.
- **Units:** Include units with a space: 20 kHz, 480 V, 200% ROI.
- **Be specific:** Never say "Save money." Say "ArcelorMittal prevented 31 hours of downtime."
- **Ban weasel words:** "Nearly," "virtually," "mostly," and "approximately" are not permitted when discussing performance. If you can't be precise, don't make the claim.

### Comparisons and Competitors

We build trust by being fair.

- Present ESA as complementary to vibration, not a replacement. ESA excels where vibration fails (submerged, hazardous, remote). Vibration is effective for high-speed, accessible equipment.
- Maintain a scientific tone. Do not disparage competitors. Highlight physics-based differences in data capture.
- Never name competitors directly on the website.

### Microcopy

Microcopy is the text at the point of action: buttons, forms, error messages, confirmations.

**Buttons:** Start with a verb. "Book a demo." "Download the whitepaper." "Talk to an engineer."

**Forms:** Use imperatives. "Enter your work email," not "Your email should be entered here."

**Errors:** Be helpful. Explain how to fix it. "This email address isn't recognized. Try using your business email."

**Success states:** Confirm and direct. "Check your inbox for the link to the whitepaper."

**Sign in**, not "Log in" (per Microsoft convention).

## Phrasebook

### Use These

| Phrase | Why |
|---|---|
| Sensorless condition monitoring | Accurate technical category name |
| Inside the MCC | Concrete, spatial. Makes the technology real. |
| Hard-to-reach assets | Names the customer's pain point |
| Electrical Signature Analysis (ESA) | The physics-based differentiator |
| Actionable insights | Moves from "data" to "value" |
| Drivetrain health | Covers the full motor-to-load path |
| Developing faults | Accurate. Avoids the "it's broken now" implication. |
| Scheduled maintenance | The positive outcome of predictive alerts |
| Energy efficiency / CO2 reduction | Executive sustainability language |
| Wired communication | Emphasizes industrial-grade security |

### Never Use These

| Banned | Why | Use Instead |
|---|---|---|
| Revolutionary / Game-changing | Looks fake. | Let results speak. |
| Best-in-class / World-leading | Arrogant and unproven. | Be specific about what makes it good. |
| Utilize | Just say "use." | Use |
| Facilitate | Bureaucratic filler. | Help, ease, allow |
| Mission-critical | Tired cliché. | Critical, essential |
| Leverage | Jargon. | Use, apply |
| Solution | Vague. Hardware? Software? Service? | Name the thing. |
| Next-generation | Meaningless. | Describe what's actually new. |
| Seamless | Nothing in industrial IT is seamless. | Integrated |
| Cutting-edge / State-of-the-art | If it's good, prove it. Don't label it. | Show evidence. |

### AI-Generated Language

Content drafted with LLMs carries a recognizable fingerprint that clashes with the Samotics voice. These words and phrases signal "AI wrote this" to attentive readers. Strip them during editing.

**Banned AI-tell words:**

| Word / Phrase | Why it fails | Use instead |
|---|---|---|
| Delve / Delve into | LLM hallmark. Nobody talks like this. | Examine, explore, look at |
| Navigate (metaphorical) | "Navigate the complexities of..." is filler. | Handle, manage, address |
| Landscape | "The industrial landscape" says nothing. | Name the specific context. |
| Harness | "Harness the power of data." Vague. | Use, apply, capture |
| Realm | "In the realm of predictive maintenance." Pompous. | In predictive maintenance |
| Foster | "Foster collaboration." Corporate LLM filler. | Build, support, encourage |
| Robust | Overused to the point of meaninglessness. | Reliable, thorough, strong (be specific) |
| Streamline | "Streamline your operations." Vague. | Name the specific improvement. |
| Bolster | "Bolster your reliability strategy." Generic. | Strengthen, improve (with specifics) |
| Tapestry / Mosaic / Myriad | Decorative filler. | Remove entirely. |
| Pivotal / Paramount | Inflated emphasis. | Important, critical (if true) |
| Multifaceted | "A multifaceted approach." Says nothing. | Name the facets. |
| Comprehensive | "A comprehensive solution." Vague. | Name what's included. |
| Embark | "Embark on your journey." Nobody embarks. | Start, begin |
| Journey | "Your digital journey." Marketing cliché. | Process, transition, shift |
| Elevate | "Elevate your maintenance." Vague verb. | Improve (with specifics) |
| It's important to note that | Filler preamble. | Delete and state the point. |
| In today's [anything] | "In today's industrial environment..." Throat-clearing. | Delete. Start with the point. |
| Arguably | Hedging filler. Either argue the point or don't. | Remove, or make the argument. |

**Pattern to watch for:** If three or more of these words appear on a single page, the content needs a rewrite, not an edit. The underlying sentence structure is likely AI-generated too.

**The test:** Read the sentence aloud. If it sounds like a LinkedIn post or a ChatGPT response, rewrite it in plain language. If a maintenance engineer at a water utility wouldn't say it out loud, it doesn't belong on the site.

## Content Templates

Each page type serves a specific audience and reading mode. Use these templates as starting structures and adapt as needed.

### Homepage

**Audience:** Executives and innovation managers scanning quickly (F-pattern).

**Structure:**
1. Hero. Name the shift: sensorless monitoring replaces manual, sensor-heavy inspection.
2. Stakes. What's at risk: uptime vs. unplanned downtime and pollution events.
3. The Promised Land. 24/7 autonomous reliability.
4. How. ESA, AI insights, MCC installation.
5. Proof. Client logos, key ROI stat.

**Tone:** Strategic and narrative. No deep technical explanations.

**Must include:** 90% accuracy claim. "Inside the MCC" value proposition.

> Example: "Traditional condition monitoring forces a trade-off: expensive manual inspections or failure-prone sensors. SAM4 eliminates this choice by using Electrical Signature Analysis (ESA) to monitor your assets directly from the MCC. This sensorless approach delivers 24/7 visibility into your most remote, submerged, and hazardous equipment."

### Category Page: Inaccessible Asset Monitoring

**Audience:** Reliability leaders and digital transformation leads scanning for alignment with their problems.

**Structure:**
1. The problem. The "inaccessible asset" blind spot.
2. The technology. Why ESA fills this gap.
3. Business outcomes. Resilience and safety.
4. Primary use cases. Submerged, hazardous, remote.

**Tone:** Visionary but grounded.

> Example: "For decades, industrial leaders have accepted a blind spot for assets in submerged, toxic, or remote environments. Traditional vibration systems cannot survive or be maintained in these locations. Sensorless monitoring fills this gap by moving the intelligence from the asset to the motor control cabinet, transforming the power signal into a diagnostic tool."

### Product Overview: SAM4

**Audience:** Maintenance managers and asset leads scanning for features.

**Structure:**
1. Value proposition. 24/7 uptime and efficiency.
2. Installation. MCC-based, no shutdown required.
3. Detection. Failure modes covered.
4. Deliverables. Dashboard, alerts, service.

**Tone:** Direct, helpful, technically credible.

**Must include:** "No batteries." "No wireless sensors on machines."

> Example: "SAM4 is a plug-and-play condition monitoring platform that requires zero sensors on the machine itself. By capturing high-frequency current and voltage data from the MCC, the system identifies 9 out of 10 developing faults up to 5 months in advance. You get actionable alerts delivered to your inbox, including specific maintenance recommendations for every detected anomaly."

### How It Works: Architecture and Physics

**Audience:** Reliability engineers and technical analysts reading deeply.

**Structure:**
1. The mechanism. How air-gap variations affect current.
2. Data acquisition. Sampling at 20 kHz.
3. Analysis. FFT, spectral analysis, lateral/torsional modes.
4. Cloud logic. AI model training and baseline creation.

**Tone:** Technically precise. Educational.

**Must include:** FFT explanation. Air-gap influence on current.

> Example: "Electrical Signature Analysis works on the principle that mechanical vibrations within a drivetrain affect the motor's magnetic field. These variations cause subtle fluctuations in the current and voltage sine waves. SAM4 captures these waveforms at 20 kHz and applies a Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) to convert time-domain data into a frequency spectrum. This allows our AI to isolate specific frequency fingerprints that correlate with mechanical and electrical failure modes."

### Industry Pages (Water, Steel, Oil and Gas)

**Audience:** Industry ops directors scanning for relevance.

**Structure:**
1. Industry pain points. Name the specific operational risks.
2. The Samotics response. Asset-specific focus.
3. Quantified outcome. Industry-relevant ROI.
4. Case study link.

**Tone:** Empathetic, expert, results-oriented.

**Must include:** Industry-specific asset names (e.g., "Archimedes screw pumps" for water, "runout table rollers" for steel).

> Example: "In the water utility sector, the failure of a submerged sewage pump isn't just a maintenance cost. It's an environmental crisis. SAM4 monitors these inaccessible assets from the MCC. By detecting pump blockages and cavitation in real time, we help you prevent pollution events and move from reactive to proactive maintenance."

### Case Studies

**Audience:** Executive buyers and engineers reading deeply for proof.

**Structure:**
1. Executive summary. The numbers, upfront.
2. The problem. Asset criticality and historical pain.
3. The detection. How SAM4 caught the fault.
4. The action. Maintenance team intervention.
5. The value. Savings in euros, hours, or CO2.

**Tone:** Restrained. Evidence-based.

**Must include:** Quantified savings. Visual evidence where available (e.g., spectral plots).

> Example: "At Nyrstar's zinc smelter, SAM4 delivered 800% ROI in 11 months. The system identified a rising intensity at the rotational frequency of a critical centrifugal pump, indicating stage-one bearing wear. By alerting the maintenance team four months before the predicted failure, we enabled repair during scheduled downtime. Without intervention, a pump seizure would have cost the plant over €150,000 in lost production."

### ESA vs. Vibration Comparison

**Audience:** Reliability engineers answering "Why not vibration?"

**Structure:**
1. Technology summary. Vibration vs. electrical.
2. Environmental fit. Asset-mounted vs. cabinet-based.
3. Fault-type strengths. Mechanical vs. electrical detection.
4. Recommendation table.

**Tone:** Balanced and fair. Scientific.

**Must include:** Installation difficulty comparison. Acknowledgment that vibration works well for accessible, high-speed assets.

> Example: "Vibration analysis is excellent for accessible assets where mechanical faults predominate. ESA offers a distinct advantage for inaccessible assets and electrical fault detection. By monitoring from the cabinet, ESA avoids the high failure rates of vibration sensors in extreme heat or submerged environments, while providing 24/7 coverage of both mechanical and electrical health."

### FAQ Page

**Audience:** Mid-to-late funnel buyers scanning for answers.

**Format:** Categorized questions (Technical, Commercial, Security). Direct answers.

**Must include:** "Does it need a shutdown?" "Who installs it?" "What's the minimum RPM?"

> Example:
> **Does SAM4 require a production shutdown for installation?**
> No. Because our sensors are non-intrusive current clamps and voltage taps, installation takes place inside the MCC without interrupting the power supply. You go live without scheduling a shutdown.

### Demo / Conversion Page

**Audience:** High-intent buyers ready to act.

**Structure:**
1. What you'll see. The value of the demo.
2. Proof. Short testimonial or stat.
3. Form. Low friction. Minimal fields.
4. Promise. No sales pressure.

**Tone:** Supportive and action-oriented.

**Must include:** "Talk to an engineer" option.

> Example: "See your assets' health in real time. In a 20-minute demo, one of our reliability specialists will show you how SAM4 detects failures in equipment like yours. We'll walk through a real dashboard, review live spectral data, and discuss your specific reliability goals. No sales pressure. Just engineering-led insights."

## Editorial Checklist

Run through this list before publishing any page.

- [ ] **Subject check:** Is the subject of every sentence a concrete noun or named entity?
- [ ] **Active voice:** Are verbs doing the work? (Minimize "is/are" constructions.)
- [ ] **Paragraph length:** Is every paragraph 4 sentences or fewer?
- [ ] **Jargon:** Is every Tier 2 acronym spelled out on first use?
- [ ] **Proof:** Does this page contain at least one specific number or client name?
- [ ] **Scan test:** Can you understand the main point by reading only the headings?
- [ ] **Curse of knowledge:** Would a non-engineer understand the mechanical benefit?
- [ ] **Banned words:** Are "revolutionary," "best-in-class," "utilize," or "seamless" present? Remove them.
- [ ] **AI language:** Are "delve," "navigate," "landscape," "harness," or other LLM-tells present? Rewrite them.
- [ ] **Em-dash check:** More than one em-dash per section? Replace with periods, colons, or commas.
- [ ] **CTA:** Does every button start with a verb?
- [ ] **Limitations:** If making a performance claim, have we stated known constraints?

### Scoring Rubric

Score each new page 0–5 on these six dimensions. Minimum average of 4 required for publication.

| Dimension | 0 (Fail) | 5 (Mastery) |
|---|---|---|
| Clarity | Complex sentences, muddy logic | Simple, direct prose |
| Proof | Vague claims without data | Quantified results with named clients |
| Scanability | Walls of text, clever-but-vague headlines | Front-loaded headings, bold keywords |
| Jargon discipline | Unexplained Tier 3 terms in high-level content | Tiered vocabulary used correctly |
| Technical credibility | Hype and marketing fluff | Engineering-led, honest about limitations |
| Conversion friction | "Click here" buttons, confusing forms | Clear CTAs, helpful error messages |

## Example Rewrites

These before/after pairs demonstrate the rules in action.

**Executive claim**

Before: "Our innovative software facilitates a paradigm shift in the way organizations approach the management of their asset lifecycles."

After: "SAM4 helps you extend asset lifespans by an average of 20% through early fault detection and performance optimization."

**Technical instruction**

Before: "It is mandatory that the installation of the current clamps be performed by a qualified electrician in accordance with the manual."

After: "A qualified electrician must install the current clamps according to the SAM4 Installation Manual."

**Sustainability**

Before: "A prioritization of energy optimization leads to a maximization of environmental sustainability metrics for your plant."

After: "SAM4 identifies energy waste at the asset level, helping your plant lower CO2 emissions and hit sustainability targets."

**Integration**

Before: "The seamless utilization of our API allows for the frictionless migration of diagnostic information into your existing CMMS."

After: "Use our REST API to push SAM4 diagnostic alerts directly into your CMMS. IBM Maximo, SAP, and others are supported."

**Cybersecurity**

Before: "We take cybersecurity very seriously and utilize state-of-the-art encryption methodologies to facilitate secure data handling."

After: "We secure your data with industry-standard encryption and wired cabinet-to-gateway communication, eliminating wireless vulnerabilities."

**AI-generated language**

Before: "Delve into the multifaceted landscape of predictive maintenance and harness the power of robust, comprehensive ESA technology to navigate the complexities of asset reliability."

After: "ESA detects mechanical and electrical faults from the motor control cabinet. No sensors on the asset. No scheduled inspections."
