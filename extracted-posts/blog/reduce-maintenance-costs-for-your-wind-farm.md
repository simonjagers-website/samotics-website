Imagine a mechanical process that has to withstand harsh conditions and is difficult to reach when there are issues. What’s more, this process relies on several industrial components working in continuous harmony. Only when every component is functioning correctly can the process itself take place.

You’ve just described the modern wind turbine.
## Maintenance is responsible for 38% of wind farm operating expenses










![](https://samotics.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/10/offshore-wind-farm-operating-expenditures_1400w.jpg) Breakdown of operational expenditures (OPEX) of an offshore wind farm, according to Renewables Advisory Board (2010). Source: Lagerveld et al. (2014)

The remote location and the vagaries of weather make the cost of turbine maintenance a hefty chunk of wind farm opex. To drive that cost down, windtechs and maintenance managers need accurate information well in advance on how turbine components are faring. A good condition monitoring solution can detect developing turbine equipment failures up to months in advance, so you can avoid the massive expense of an emergency trip to repair a turbine that’s down. This degree of advance notice also means you no longer need to preventively replace components that may still have months or years of failure-free life. Add to that the fact that catching faults early prolongs a machine’s usable lifespan, and condition monitoring enables you to spend significantly less on personnel and hardware while maximizing turbine uptime.
## Many equipment failures, many monitoring solutions


Wind turbine parts are a varied bunch: glass or carbon fiber blades, steel pitch and yaw gears, copper wiring in the generator. From blade cracks to bearing degradation, each fault carries its own fingerprint of failure—and each condition monitoring technology will detect certain problems sooner than others. How do you choose the right technique for each potential problem?
