The situation

What was at stake

Borehole pumps are the pumping heart of the water industry — they supply clean water to residential areas. They are also notoriously difficult to monitor: submerged up to 200 metres below ground, in remote locations, impossible to reach with traditional vibration sensors. Lifting a pump for inspection is expensive and time-consuming. Without remote monitoring, faults go undetected until the pump fails.

What SAM4 detected

The signal and the diagnosis

SAM4 installs in the motor control cabinet — not on the pump — making it uniquely suited for submerged assets. Across multiple water utility deployments, SAM4 has detected: (1) clogging from iron buildup, flagged nearly a year in advance through decreasing active power and cavitation signatures; (2) VFD irregularities, where a pump was overshooting maximum current due to misconfigured inverter settings; and (3) mechanical unbalance, detected through a sudden increase in spectral energy at the rotational frequency. In each case, maintenance teams acted on the alert and confirmed the diagnosis.

The outcome

What happened next

Every detection prevented either pump failure, supply disruption, or both. SAM4's ability to monitor from the MCC eliminates the barrier that makes borehole pumps difficult to maintain. Detectable faults include clogging, grid and VFD irregularities, bearing degradation, and various forms of mechanical wear.

“This technology is ideal for borehole pumps. They can be 150–180 metres below ground, so having to fit a monitor to that borehole pump would be impossible. Having SAM4 in the MCC is easy to set up and easy to maintain.”

James Nobbs Groundwater Engineering Manager, Anglian Water
