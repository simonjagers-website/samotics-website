The Proximity Penalty

The gap isn't negligence.
It's physics.

Traditional condition monitoring requires a sensor on or near the asset. For 30–40% of your critical rotating equipment, that's physically impossible, economically irrational, or regulatory prohibited. The result: a structural blind spot no amount of effort can close — unless you change the method.

Three structural barriers

Why sensors can't reach these assets

Every barrier below is structural. Better planning won't fix it. A bigger maintenance budget won't fix it. The only way to monitor these assets is to change where the sensor sits.

01

Physical access

Vibration sensors, temperature probes, and acoustic monitors all share one requirement: physical proximity to the rotating element. Mount them on the bearing housing, the motor frame, or the gearbox casing. When the asset is submerged, sealed, or operating in a hostile environment, that proximity is impossible to maintain.

Submerged sewage pumps

Sit 5–20 metres underwater in wet wells. To inspect or mount a sensor, you drain the well, extract the pump, and shut down the station. Most utilities run thousands of these across hundreds of remote sites.

Water & Wastewater

Enclosed compressors

Run inside sealed, acoustically insulated housings. Accessing the motor bearing requires partial disassembly — which means a production shutdown and specialist labour.

Chemicals & Petrochemicals

High-temperature motors

Operate in environments above 60°C. Adhesive-mounted accelerometers degrade and detach within months. Stud-mounted sensors survive but require machined surfaces — often impractical on legacy equipment.

Metals & Mining
02

Economics

The sensor itself is cheap. Everything around it is not. For hard-to-reach assets, the installation cost dwarfs the hardware cost by 10× or more. At that price, the monitoring business case fails — and the asset stays dark.

Sensor hardware $500
Installed on hard-to-reach asset $5,000+

Scaffolding and access equipment

Elevated motors, column-mounted pumps, and overhead fans require scaffolding or aerial platforms. Rental, setup, and teardown add days and thousands in cost per asset.

Production shutdowns

Many sensors must be mounted while the machine is stationary. Scheduling downtime for sensor installation competes with production targets — and often loses.

Specialist labour

Confined space entry, ATEX-rated installations, and submerged pump extraction require certified personnel. The labour cost per asset can exceed the sensor cost by 5–10×.

03

Regulation

Even when physical access exists and the budget is available, regulation adds another layer of friction. In hazardous areas, every installation must be certified, every entry must be permitted, and every procedure must be documented. This overhead makes routine sensor deployment impractical at scale.

ATEX zone requirements

Any equipment installed in an ATEX-classified zone must be explosion-proof certified. The sensor, the cable, the junction box — everything. Certification adds weeks of lead time and thousands in cost per installation.

Oil & Gas · Chemicals

Confined space entry permits

Accessing a submerged pump or enclosed motor requires a confined space permit, atmospheric testing, a standby rescue team, and a safety supervisor. For a single sensor installation, the regulatory overhead can consume an entire shift.

Water · Metals & Mining

Hot work and isolation procedures

Welding stud mounts onto motor frames in operational areas triggers hot work permits, fire watches, and lockout/tagout procedures. Each installation becomes a mini-project with its own safety paperwork.

All heavy industry

The reframe

This isn't a maintenance problem. It's a method problem.

Better maintenance practices, bigger budgets, and more frequent inspections won't close this gap. The barrier is the monitoring method itself: sensors that require proximity to the asset.

The 30–40% of assets that go unmonitored aren't forgotten. Reliability teams know exactly which pumps, compressors, and fans sit outside their monitoring programme. They know the risk. They've calculated the cost. But as long as the method demands a sensor on the machine, the economics, the physics, and the regulation will keep those assets dark.

Closing the gap requires a fundamentally different approach: one that moves the measurement point away from the asset entirely.
