## Voice Guidelines

### How we write and speak

Our voice should feel like a knowledgeable colleague — someone who's been on site, understands the pressure, and speaks in terms the audience actually uses. Not a vendor reading from a slide deck.

**Use active voice.** "SAM4 detected a bearing fault" — not "A bearing fault was detected by the SAM4 platform."

**Be specific.** Concrete examples and real numbers beat abstract claims. "We monitor 10,000+ assets across 20+ countries" lands harder than "We're a global leader."

**Match the audience.** Reliability engineers want technical depth. CFOs want cost impact. Adjust detail, not tone.

**Write for a multilingual audience.** Our customer base spans 20+ countries and at least six languages. Many users read English as a second language. Keep sentences clean, avoid idioms that don't translate, and provide local-language support where possible. If we promise a personal touch, that has to work in German, Dutch, French, and Spanish — not just English.

**Respect the buyer.** These are experienced professionals. They've seen vendor pitches come and go. Don't talk down. Don't hype. Earn trust with substance.

### Phrases we use

- "Hard-to-reach assets" or "inaccessible assets"
- "The assets and faults that matter most" (reinforces our focused detection strategy)
- "Electrical Signature Analysis" (always spell out on first use, then ESA)
- "Actionable insights" (but only when we mean it — tied to a specific action)
- "Proactive maintenance" (as opposed to reactive/run-to-fail)
- "You don't know what you don't know" (a phrase our customers use themselves)

### Phrases we avoid

- "World-class" / "best-in-class" (empty without proof)
- "Cutting-edge" / "revolutionary" (overused, sounds like every other vendor)
- "Synergy" / "paradigm shift" (corporate filler)
- "Smart" as a prefix for everything (imprecise)
- "Digital transformation" as a standalone value prop (too vague — say what it actually does)

## Target Audience Snapshot

**Primary:** Maintenance professionals and reliability engineers (mostly 40+, mostly male, global but concentrated in UK/Europe)

**Secondary:** Production managers and financial decision-makers who approve budgets

**Their world:** They own the reliability of their plant. They're measured on uptime, cost control, and compliance. They carry the weight when something fails. They value honesty, technical competence, and partners who show up in person.

**Their pain:**
- Unexpected equipment failures causing unplanned downtime
- Blind spots in their monitoring coverage — assets they can't reach with traditional sensors
- Pressure to justify maintenance spend to leadership who see it as a cost center
- The nagging feeling that something's about to go wrong and they can't see it
- Long ramp-up time before any monitoring solution proves its value — they've been burned by vendors who promise instant results

**What earns their trust:**
- Showing up on site
- Being honest about what the technology can and can't do
- Providing references they can actually call
- Dependable communication — no disappearing acts
- Quick wins that build to long-term value
