Buyer Journey Audit

6 industry pages simulated as a buyer — entry to exit. What works, what drops, what to fix.

14 Issues found

Cross-page issues (affect all or most journeys)

P1 "How it works" section is word-for-word identical on all 6 pages. A buyer who visits two pages will notice the copy-paste. The 3-step install flow is universal, but the lead sentence and step descriptions could reference industry-specific context (e.g. "no confined-space entry in wastewater pits" vs "no nacelle access required").
P1 Trust bar and proof callout name different companies. Chemicals mentions Kuraray and Nobian in the proof callout — but neither is in the trust bar (Simon removed them). Metals mentions ArcelorMittal in the proof callout and case studies — but ArcelorMittal isn't in the trust bar. This creates a credibility gap: "you cite them as proof, but won't put their logo up?"
P2 No objection handling anywhere. Zero pages address the two questions every buyer asks: (1) "How is this different from vibration monitoring?" and (2) "We already have SCADA — what's new?" A short ESA-vs-vibration comparison block (even 2 sentences) would prevent drop-off at the "sounds too good to be true" moment.
P2 No customer quotes or testimonials. Case studies exist, but there isn't a single direct quote from a customer on any page. One quote per page — from a named person at a named company — would massively increase trust.
P3 CTA block and pre-footer feel redundant. Two conversion sections back-to-back at the bottom of every page. On Water, they have the identical heading. Consider: make the CTA block the primary conversion point (mid-links + CTAs), and strip the pre-footer down to just the ROI calculator link — or remove it entirely.

2. Energy Production

Needs work

Buyer persona: Reliability engineer at an oil major or wind farm operator. Landed from LinkedIn or a direct referral.

Hero "Monitor the assets that keep energy flowing" — generic. A Shell reliability engineer doesn't think about "keeping energy flowing." They think about compressor uptime or ESP run life. The lead text covers too many sub-sectors in one breath (oil rigs, gas compressors, wind turbines, thermal plants). !
Proof callout "95% detection rate measured across the full SAM4 fleet" — not energy-specific. Honest, but doesn't give the buyer a reason to believe this works in their world. !
Challenges 6 cards — most of any page. Good breadth (ATEX, ESP, wind, thermal, downtime, seawater). Two have SPE paper links. But 6 cards may overwhelm — the buyer's eye has to work harder. ✓
Results All fleet-wide numbers. Zero energy-specific KPIs. The disclaimer note admits this. A buyer reads: they haven't proven this in my industry yet. ✗
Case studies Only 2 (after Sabesp removal). One ESP, one oil transfer pump. No wind, no thermal, no compressor case. Thinnest proof section of all pages. ✗
CTA → Exit SAM4-X validation offer is good — a low-commitment entry point for a sceptical buyer. But the journey to get there feels thin on proof. !
Only 2 case studies: All other pages have 3. Consider adding a generic "condition monitoring on offshore pumps" or similar to fill the gap, or restructure as 2 cards at larger size.
Hero headline is generic: Tighten to hit a specific pain. E.g. "Detect faults in the assets your sensors can't reach" — which is what the lead text already says better.
Results section has no energy-specific data: Even "2 ESP failures detected" would be better than fleet-wide stats. If data exists from Jira, consider surfacing it.
Pre-footer sub still says "motor fleet": Should match the industry-specific pattern used on other pages.

6. Pulp & Paper

Thinnest journey

Buyer persona: Mill maintenance lead at a European paper manufacturer. Landed from a Google search for "paper mill predictive maintenance."

Hero "Keep your paper mills running efficiently" — "efficiently" is vague and weak. Compare to Metals ("Keep your mills running and your mines dewatered") which is concrete. The buyer doesn't feel urgency. !
Proof callout "€96k in downtime costs saved at Pfleiderer" — decent, specific. But only one data point. !
Trust bar Only 2 logos: Pfleiderer and Sappi. Looks thin. A competitor page with 6 logos wins the visual trust test. !
Results All fleet-wide stats (95% detection, 5 months, 5-35% energy waste, 20 min install). Zero pulp & paper-specific numbers. Buyer thinks: this is the same page as everyone else. ✗
Case studies Only 1 of 3 is named (Pfleiderer fans). The other two ("belt-driven equipment", "centrifugal pump") are generic. A paper mill buyer wants to see paper mill proof. !
Hero headline needs urgency: Replace "efficiently" with something concrete. E.g. "Detect faults before they stop the paper machine" or "Keep your paper machines running — no sensors in the mill."
Results section has zero industry-specific data: If Pfleiderer data exists (€96k, 6 fans, X weeks warning), surface it as named stats.
2 of 3 case studies are generic: Consider whether the generic ones help or hurt. A page with 1 strong named case study might be better than 3 where 2 are filler.
Trust bar looks thin with 2 logos: Consider removing it entirely and using a hero proof callout pattern instead (like Airports). Or add more if names become available.
